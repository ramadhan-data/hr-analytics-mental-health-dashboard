## Business Recommendations

### 1. Prioritize Overtime Reduction in High-Risk Industries
Finance & Banking, Consulting, Technology, and Legal show burnout scores 40-55% higher than 
the lowest-risk industries (Education, Manufacturing). This gap is explained almost entirely 
by workload: these industries average ~49 work hours and ~10 overtime hours per week, compared 
to ~41 hours and ~3.5 overtime hours elsewhere. 
**Action**: Set a maximum overtime cap (e.g., 5-6 hours/week) specifically for these four 
industries, and monitor burnout scores quarterly to measure impact.

### 2. Don't Rely on Manager Support Training as a Burnout Solution
Manager support score shows virtually no correlation with burnout (r = 0.003), even after 
testing for non-linear effects. While manager support remains valuable for other outcomes 
(team morale, retention), it should not be the centerpiece of a burnout-reduction strategy.
**Action**: Redirect budget from generic "manager support" training toward workload management 
policies (e.g., overtime caps, workload redistribution tools).

### 3. Audit EAP Utilization, Not Just Availability
Among employees with EAP access, 68% have never used it. Companies with a mental health 
policy or EAP show no measurable improvement in burnout, satisfaction, or work-life balance 
compared to those without — suggesting the programs exist but aren't reaching employees.
**Action**: Conduct an internal survey to identify barriers to EAP usage (awareness, stigma, 
accessibility, trust) before investing in new wellness programs.

### 4. Expand Remote Work Options Where Feasible
Remote employees show meaningfully lower burnout (3.82) than Hybrid (4.14) or On-site (4.08) 
employees, with no measurable drop in productivity or job satisfaction. Notably, Hybrid workers 
report the highest burnout of all three models — possibly due to the added complexity of 
switching between environments.
**Action**: For roles where remote work is operationally viable, consider expanding remote 
options as a low-cost burnout mitigation strategy. Re-evaluate hybrid scheduling policies 
specifically, given their unexpectedly high burnout association.

### 5. Treat Burnout and Retention as Separate Problems
Burnout shows almost no correlation with intention to leave (r = 0.0075). This means 
burnout-reduction initiatives should not be marketed or measured as retention strategies — 
they serve a different purpose (employee wellbeing and long-term productivity) and require 
separate KPIs. Retention drivers should be investigated independently (e.g., compensation, 
career growth, job market conditions).

### 6. Focus Wellbeing Initiatives on Workload, Not Peripheral Programs
Work-life balance is the strongest protective factor against burnout (r = -0.48), but has 
no measurable effect on productivity or job satisfaction. This confirms that the most direct 
lever for reducing burnout is controlling workload and hours — not indirect wellness 
initiatives, which show negligible impact across this dataset.
